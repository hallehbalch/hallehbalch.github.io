Research -- 2D Lattices
Emerging physics in 2D organic lattices
Research Collaboration: Feng Wang (Berkeley), Will Dichtel (Northwestern), Seth Marder (Georgia Tech), Jean-Luc Bredas (Georgia Tech / Arizona), Dan Ralph (Cornell), Mike Crommie (Berkeley)

For the past decade, control of low-dimensional materials (like graphene and transition metal dichalcogenides) and their heterostructures has enabled observation of both new quantum phenomena and efficient nanoscale technologies spanning solar energy conversion to water purification. Consequently, the ability to tune the electronic and optical properties of a low-dimensional material is of substantial research interest. Organic molecular lattices present a compelling route, where the electronic band structure can, in principle, be designed through choice of constituent molecules and formed using the tools of synthetic chemistry. From a broader perspective, organic lattices formed of naturally abundant atoms (e.g. C, N, O, H) represent an idealized experimental platform to explore electronic charge and energy transfer central to many complex and dynamic systems on Earth.

For example, we can ask "what are the design principles that control the flow of electrons in molecular lattices? Can we demonstrate such control through rational selection of the constituent molecules and how they are bound to one another? In doing so, can we develop new optical emitters and flexible devices?" To address these questions, we studied the underlying principles that control the formation of dispersive and flat electronic bands in 2D organic frameworks. Where topologically nontrivial flat bands can provide rich opportunities for control of strongly correlated electronic states, low-bandgap dispersive bands can provide optically luminescent, and electronically addressable materials with tunable charge dynamics in nanoscale devices.

I approach these questions using optical spectroscopy, photoluminescence micrsocopy, AFM, high-resolution TEM, and X-ray scattering both in home-built setups and at the Advanced Light Source (ALS) and the Molecular Foundry at the Lawrence Berkeley National Lab.
