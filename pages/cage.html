Research -- 2D Lattices

Photonics for imaging electric fields
Research Collaboration: Feng Wang (Berkeley), Bianxiao Cui (Stanford), Mike Crommie (Berkeley)

Electrical activity at molecular length scales is central to living systems. From movement to memory, the ability of living systems to respond to environmental cues is grounded in electrical signaling between cells– where charge and energy transfer between molecules enables information to be integrated and transmitted across much longer length scales.

For exmaple, fundamental questions in bioelectricity are grounded in understanding how electrical signalling from a single cell is transmitted and integrated across the entire cellular network. Consequently, there are both scientific and technical opportunities in the development of methods to non-perturbatively record the dynamics of microvolt local electric field distributions generated by single-cell action potentials with sub-millisecond resolution.

To address these questions, we developed new photonic devices that leverge the strong light-matter interactions in graphene to convert electric fields into optical images. Atomically thin materials, like graphene, are exceptionally sensitive to their local environment, couple strongly to light, and can be strongly modulated through electrostatic gating. fabricating, and demonstrating a completely new photonic devices that leverages the optical transitions from Pauli blocking in graphene to convert electric fields into optical images. This new technology enables us to directly visualize the flow of electric fields in biological and chemical reactions: dynamics which are ubiquitous but unobserved. I saw this project from the start to finish, from developing the python simulation, building the setup, to applying the new sensor to image action potentials without labels or dyes in spontaneously firing cardiac networks.
