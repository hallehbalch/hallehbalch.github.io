Research

I am interested in understanding how long-range behavior emerges from the interactions of electrons at molecular length scales. My research focuses on fundamental questions about the flow of charge and energy at the nanoscale, which play a central role throughout the natural world in processes spanning photosynthesis to bioelectricity. Research into these fundamental interactions can give rise to the discovery of novel functional materials and to the invention of new technologies.

To study these systems, I develop and use a wide range of imaging, optical spectroscopy, and scanning probe techniques that leverage broadband energies, polarization control, with high spatiotemporal resolution.

Emerging physics in 2D organic lattices
         For the past decade, control of low-dimensional materials (like graphene and transition metal dichalcogenides) and their heterostructures has enabled observation of both new quantum phenomena and efficient nanoscale technologies spanning solar energy conversion to water purification. Consequently, the ability to tune the electronic and optical properties of a low-dimensional material is of substantial research interest. Organic molecular lattices present a compelling route, where the electronic band structure can, in principle, be designed through choice of constituent molecules and formed using the tools of synthetic chemistry. From a broader perspective, organic lattices formed of naturally abundant atoms (e.g. C, N, O, H) represent an idealized experimental platform to explore electronic charge and energy transfer in more complex and dynamic systems on earth.
         For example, we can ask "what are the design principles that control the flow of electrons in molecular lattices by careful selection of the constituent molecules and how they are bound to one another? Can we develop new optical emitters and flexible devices?" To do so, we try to understand and control the formation of dispersive and flat electronic bands in 2D organic frameworks. Where topologically nontrivial flat bands can provide rich opportunities for control of strongly correlated electronic states, low-bandgap dispersive bands can provide optically luminescent, and electronically addressable materials with tunable charge dynamics in nanoscale devices.
        I approach these questions using optical spectroscopy, photoluminescence micrsocopy, AFM, high-resolution TEM, and X-ray scattering both in home-built setups and at the Advanced Light Source (ALS) and the Molecular Foundry at the Lawrence Berkeley National Lab.
